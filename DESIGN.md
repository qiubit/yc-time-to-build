## Problem Statement

Aspiring founders don't know when to build what. They wonder if they're too early, too late, or whether the current moment is actually special — or just feels that way. There's no piece of writing that makes the case *with data*, using 20 years of YC batch history as the evidence base.

The generative AI / agent wave appears to be one of the fastest and broadest founder-attention waves in YC history. That claim needs proof, not assertion. YC batch composition is a 20-year leading indicator of where ambitious founders bet their careers. The data shows something extraordinary happening right now, but the durable outcome question is still open: does AI / agents mature like fintech and commerce, or stay narrower like crypto?

## What Makes This Cool

The "whoa" is not "look at all these waves." It's: **"YC history has seen real founder waves before. AI / agents is different because it is both a startup wave of its own and a building block for many other kinds of startups."**

The essay builds to one specific, defensible claim: YC data shows generative AI / agents becoming unusually fast and cross-sector in founder attention — commerce, fintech, and crypto included as comparators. Every wave chart, every historical comparison, every interactive moment in the essay exists to support that claim while staying honest about what batch composition cannot yet prove.

The virality trigger is not data curiosity — it's permission. "This convinced me to quit my job." "This is the thing I'm sending everyone who asks me if they should start something." That's the tweet you're designing toward.

The medium is an **interactive visual essay** (explorable explanation / scrollytelling microsite) in the tradition of The Pudding, Nicky Case, and Bartosz Ciechanowski. The story IS the product. The YC wave chart is one piece of evidence in a larger argument, not the subject itself. The essay ends with a concrete call to action: apply to YC, start building now, here's why.

## Data Model

Batch-level aggregated JSON (baked into the page at build time, not per-company). Target payload: < 50KB compressed.

```json
{ "batch": "S21", "year": 2021, "season": "summer", "wave": "Fintech", "count": 42, "pct": 0.18, "total_companies": 233 }
```

Canonical wave categories for v1: Web / Social / UGC, Mobile-Enabled Commerce / Marketplaces, Fintech, Crypto / Web3, Generative AI / Agents, Other.

Canonical background sectors (~20-30, used for cross-sector spread and optional appendix): AI/ML, Generative AI / Agents, Crypto/Web3, SaaS/B2B, Consumer, Fintech, Healthcare, EdTech, Developer Tools, Climate/Sustainability, E-commerce, Security, Biotech, Hardware, Media/Entertainment, Gaming, Space, Transportation, Marketplace, Government/Defense, Other.

**Wave definitions — v1 starting point:**

- **Web / Social / UGC (2005-2011):** social, content, media, messaging, chat, reviews, user-generated web. Useful as the prologue; early batch sizes are small and noisy.
- **Mobile-Enabled Commerce / Marketplaces (2009-2016):** e-commerce, marketplaces, grocery, delivery, logistics, retail tech, restaurant tech, travel/local services, and smartphone-enabled on-demand behavior. This is the strongest historical comparator because it produced durable YC outcomes and shows a real platform shift.
- **Fintech (2014-2022):** fintech, payments, lending, banking, neobanks, payroll, insurance, asset management, investing. Use as the durable-company benchmark.
- **Crypto / Web3 (2017-2022):** crypto, blockchain, Web3, DeFi, NFT, DAO. Use as the narrowness benchmark. It produced Coinbase, but likely not a broad durable-company wave inside YC.
- **Generative AI / Agents (2023-present):** generative AI, AI assistants, agents, copilots, LLM apps, AI ops, automation, voice AI, agent infrastructure. Keep distinct from older ML / AI companies such as computer vision, autonomous vehicles, ML infra, and drug discovery.

**Category taxonomy — v1 prerequisite:** A manual mapping of YC's inconsistent historical tags to the canonical waves above. 3-5 hours. This is different from the backlog item "NLP-assisted taxonomy normalization" (which refines edge cases at scale); the manual map is the one-time baseline that makes the wave chart possible at all. Batches with zero entries for a wave are plotted as 0%, not omitted. Data gaps before W08 (early YC era, <9 companies/batch) are annotated on the timeline rather than extrapolated.

**Methodology caveat:** YC tags and one-liners are current/public company-profile metadata, not a frozen record of what each company called itself during its YC batch. The essay must disclose this. V1 should include the sensitivity check validated in the spike: strict tag/industry classification vs. broader tag/industry + one-liner/description keyword classification. Strict mode gives the conservative lower bound; broad mode captures the current AI / agents signal but must be caveated for metadata drift.

Estimated payload: ~800-1200 rows (20-30 categories × ~40 batches) × ~120 bytes each = ~100-150KB uncompressed. Gzip compression (~70%) yields ~30-45KB. Target < 50KB compressed is achievable; do not include per-company lists in the baked JSON (lazy-load or omit in v1).

## Premises

1. YC batch composition is a meaningful **leading indicator** of founder attention and technology wave timing — ambitious founders apply before the market has fully settled what the wave means.
2. YC batch composition does **not** prove "biggest opportunity in startup history" by itself. It can show founder attention, speed, concentration, and cross-sector spread. Durable opportunity requires separate evidence: outcomes, revenue, valuations, funding, or public/exited/top-company status.
3. The core artifact is a **thesis-driven essay with data as evidence**, not a wave explorer or company search tool. The thesis: "YC history shows several founder waves. Generative AI / agents is different because it is both a startup wave and a building block for many other kinds of startups."
4. Twitter virality requires one **embeddable, self-contained visual moment** — a screenshot or clip that works as a tweet before anyone clicks a link. For this essay, that moment is the chart comparing the AI wave's growth curve to prior waves — not neutral, not open-ended, but visually making the case.
5. The **story comes first**. The thesis, the narrative arc, and the final call to action are the load-bearing pieces. The wave chart is evidence. The AI agent / deeper exploration is a fork at the end, not the entry point.
6. Use **ExploreYC as product inspiration and `yc-oss/api` as the reproducible evidence layer**. Revisit ExploreYC only if it later exposes a stable open export with clearer normalized data.
7. The honest unresolved question is the **outcome fork**: AI / agents has strong founder-formation evidence now, but the available public outcome proxies cannot yet say whether it will mature like fintech/commerce or stay narrower like crypto.

## Validated Wave Set

Use these waves in v1:

1. **Web / Social / UGC, 2005-2011.** Prologue wave. It helps show YC's original "tiny teams can build web apps" era, but batch sizes are small, so do not lean on it for hard statistical claims.
2. **Mobile-Enabled Commerce / Marketplaces, 2009-2016.** Main historical comparator. This captures Airbnb, Instacart, DoorDash, Flexport, ShipBob, GOAT-style marketplace and commerce behavior. It is stronger than "mobile" alone because the companies that mattered were usually not tagged as mobile; they were commerce, marketplace, logistics, delivery, or local-services companies enabled by smartphones.
3. **Fintech, 2014-2022.** Durable-company comparator. Strong YC outcomes and measurable tags: payments, banking, lending, payroll, neobanks, insurance, investing.
4. **Crypto / Web3, 2017-2022.** Narrowness comparator. Coinbase is a huge YC outcome, but crypto appears narrow in YC batch composition compared with commerce, fintech, and AI. Use it to show that not every loud wave becomes a broad YC company-formation wave; avoid claiming that the simple public outcome proxies prove crypto "failed."
5. **Generative AI / Agents, 2023-present.** Thesis wave. It must be measured separately from older ML / AI and tested for cross-sector spread. The spike supports an unusually strong founder-attention signal, but not mature outcome proof yet.

Do **not** use SaaS as a headline wave. SaaS / B2B is the background substrate of modern YC, not a clean "rise, peak, fade" wave. Use it as context: YC's top companies and recent batches are heavily B2B software, but that is not the same as a discrete founder wave.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Static Scrollytelling HTML Essay (Selected)
One self-contained HTML page. YC wave data scraped once and compiled to aggregated JSON (< 50KB compressed, batch-level not per-company). D3.js indexed growth curves as the primary proof chart, with stacked area and small multiples as supporting context, embedded in a scroll-guided narrative using **Scrollama.js** for scroll event binding (avoids reinventing IntersectionObserver plumbing). No JS component framework (React/Vue); D3.js + a minimal `npm run build` script is sufficient. No backend. Deploys to GitHub Pages. `og:image` (1200×630px) generated at build time via a Playwright headless screenshot of the wave chart (Playwright is a dev-only dependency, not included in page weight). S = 1-2 weekends. Risk: Low.

### Approach B: Interactive Essay + Live AI Agent Fork
Same scrollytelling structure + AI agent at the end (Claude API + FastAPI backend) that lets readers ask questions about any wave and generate on-demand visualizations. Full vision in one release. Effort: L (3-4 weeks). Risk: Medium — backend investment before validating the essay resonates.

### Approach C: Observable Notebook-First
Write the wave essay as a reactive, forkable Observable notebook. Fastest prototype; built-in community shareability. Less visual control over scroll choreography and layout. Effort: S (1 weekend). Risk: Low.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach A.** Build the static scrollytelling essay first. Validate that the wave-timing story is actually compelling enough to share before investing in backend infrastructure. The AI agent and deeper exploration features go into the backlog. If the essay lands (someone shares it, people say "whoa"), Approach B becomes the roadmap.

## Proposed Essay Arc (draft — to be finalized in Step 2)

1. **Hook:** "YC history has seen founder waves before. This one is moving faster." (Full-bleed, minimal text, wave animation starts)
2. **The durable waves:** Walk through mobile-enabled commerce / marketplaces and fintech using YC batch composition plus lightweight outcome proxies. Show how real platform shifts created durable companies, while disclosing that outcomes require lagging evidence.
3. **The cautionary wave:** Show crypto/Web3. It was loud, important, and produced Coinbase, but inside YC it appears narrower than its cultural footprint. This makes the reader trust the essay because the chart is allowed to complicate the story.
4. **The current wave:** Zoom into generative AI / agents. Show the indexed growth curve and then split it by sector. The claim is not just "more AI companies." It is "AI / agents is both its own startup wave and a building block inside many other kinds of startups."
5. **The fork:** Put the uncertainty on screen. AI / agents has strong formation evidence now; whether it becomes more like fintech/commerce or more like crypto requires outcome evidence over time.
6. **The call:** "The window is open. It won't be for long. Here's how to get started." Link to YC application, link to deeper exploration.
